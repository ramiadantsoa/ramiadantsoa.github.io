About

Tanjona Ramiadantsoa

My main research topic is to understand how environmental degradation namely habitat destruction and global warming affects biodiversity. A major empirical motivation is the patterns of loss and fragmentation of natural forest in Madagascar and their consequences on its unique biodiversity. I mainly use and develop mathematical models based on physiological, ecological, and evolutionary principle to understand the pattern and process of biodiversity loss.

I obtained a MSc in applied mathematics under the supervision of Arthur Randrianarivony at the University of Antananarivo in Madagascar. Afterwards, I did a PhD with Ilkka Hanksi and Otso Ovaskainen at the University of Helsinki in Finland followed by a postdoc with Emma Goldberg at the University of Minnesota, USA. Currently, I am doing a second postdoc at the University of Wisconsin-Madison working on Abrupt Changes in Ecological System. It is multidisciplinary project lead by Monica Turner and involves three postdocs: Zak Ratajczak, Allison Stegner, Jien Zhang, and four coPIs: Steve Carpenter, Anthony Ives, Chris Kucharick, and Jack Williams.

Specialist's extinction threshold

Species with narrow niche breadth are expected to be most vulnerable to habitat destruction. The minimum amount of habitat necessary for the persistence of species is called extinction threshold. My research involve understanding factors that influence species' extinction threshold and the waiting time until actual extinction. I explore to understand how the extinction threshold changes as a function of endogeneous processes such as niche breadth, niche overlap, types of competition, dispersal ability, and exogeneous processes such as the rate of habitat loss, pattern and process of habitat fragmentation, of local and regional stochasticity. More generally, this is about generating an 'general' species area relationship by adding more assumptions.

Publications:   Ramiadantsoa, T., Hanski I., and Ovaskainen, O. (under review) Responses of Generalist and Specialist Species to Fragmented Landscapes.

Body size and thermal performance curve

The thermal performance curve describes the performance of a species/population/individual as a function of ambient temperature. Knowing the thermal niche can be essential in understanding how species/population/individual can be affected by warming (or cooling) environment. Despite the ubiquitous of body size and temperature, its role in shaping thermal performance curve is unknown. I use energy budget theory to understand how thermodynamical, physiological, and ecological processes. More generally, here we construct a generalized fitness landscape with body size and temperature as explanatory variables.

Phylogeography and radiation

Heterogeneous environment tends to harbor higher biodiversity as, intuitively, it allows to pack a large number of specialist species. Most of my work involves developing statistical method to find correlation between spatial and temporal heterogeneities and diversification rates. My work extends existing phylogenetic comparative methods for geographic distribution (and more generally discrete character states) to accommodate for more elaborate macroevolutionary model. I rely on Approximate Bayesian Computation to fit model to data. The use of ABC is also a challenge to expand summary statistics for model adequacy.
